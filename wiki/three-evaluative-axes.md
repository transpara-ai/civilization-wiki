# Three Independent Evaluative Axes

**Three ways of judging that the framework refuses to collapse.** The Three Independent Evaluative Axes are the claim that value has (at least) three irreducible dimensions — **Practical** (*is it useful?*), **Moral** (*is it right?*), and **Aesthetic** (*is it beautiful?*) — and that no single number can stand in for all three. They enter the arc on **2026-02-28** in the second Searles post, *"From 44 to 200"* ([Searles-P2]), as a short structural coda placed immediately after [[the-permanent-tensions|The Permanent Tensions]] and the [[three-irreducibles|three things the framework can't derive]]. The source's framing: *"The framework also surfaces three ways of judging that can't be reduced to each other."*

The load-bearing sentence — the one the wiki should not soften — is the closing claim: *"Any system that collapses these into a single metric — 'utility' or 'alignment' or 'optimisation' — is lying about the structure of value."* This is the passage's direct shot at single-objective optimisation: the assertion that a value model with one scalar output is not a simplification but a misrepresentation.

## The three axes

Each axis is tied by the same post to a specific primitive at a specific [[fourteen-layers|layer]]. The source lists them tersely ([Searles-P2]):

- **Practical** — *"is it useful?"* Tied to the primitive **Value**, at **Layer 1 (Agency)**.
- **Moral** — *"is it right?"* Tied to **Ethics**, at **Layer 7 (Ethics)**.
- **Aesthetic** — *"is it beautiful?"* Tied to the primitive **Aesthetic**, at **Layer 11 (Culture)**.

The independence is illustrated, not derived, by three short mismatches the post offers as proof-by-example that the axes come apart:

> *"Something can be useful but wrong. Something can be right but ugly. Something can be beautiful but useless."*

Each clause holds one axis high while another goes low — the demonstration that a high score on one axis predicts nothing about the others, which is what *"independent"* is meant to assert.

> ⚠ **Fail-legible note (one layer name is parenthetical, not the layer's own name).** The source writes the axes as *"Practical (Value, Layer 1)"*, *"Moral (Ethics, Layer 7)"*, and *"Aesthetic (Aesthetic, Layer 11)."* All three primitive-to-layer attributions check out verbatim against the same post's *"The Layers"* section: **Layer 1's primitive list opens with *Value*** (the layer itself is named **Agency**, not "Practical" — the post pairs the *Practical axis* with the *Value primitive*, not with a layer called "Practical"); **Layer 7 (Ethics)** is the ethics layer; and **Layer 11 (Culture)** lists *Aesthetic* among its primitives. The axis labels (Practical / Moral / Aesthetic) are the post's names for the *kinds of judgement*; the parentheticals are the *primitives and layers* they draw on. The wiki keeps the two straight rather than implying a one-to-one layer-name match.

## What "independent" is doing here

The word doing the work is *independent*. The claim is not merely that the three judgements are different questions, but that they are **orthogonal** — that you cannot trade one off against the others on a common scale, because there is no common scale. This is why the passage targets the *collapse* into a single metric specifically: reducing value to one number ("utility," "alignment," "optimisation") forces a conversion rate between usefulness, rightness, and beauty that the source says does not exist.

> ⚠ **Fail-legible note (orthogonality is asserted and illustrated, not proven).** The independence claim is established in the source by **three illustrative examples** (useful-but-wrong, right-but-ugly, beautiful-but-useless), not by argument that the three axes are *in general* uncorrelated or formally incommensurable. The examples show the axes *can* diverge in particular cases; they do not prove the axes are *always* independent, nor that no aggregate metric could ever be legitimate. Treat "three independent axes" as the author's **design stance**, supported by existence-proof examples — not as a demonstrated theorem about the structure of value.

## The claim against single-metric optimisation

The passage's sharp edge is its naming of three real targets: *"utility," "alignment," "optimisation."* In context this is a claim about value models that reduce everything worth caring about to one scalar to be maximised — and the source's verdict is unusually blunt: such a system is not approximating value imperfectly, it is *"lying about the structure of value."*

> ⚠ **Fail-legible note (this is a philosophical assertion, not a proven result).** That a single-metric value model is *"lying"* is the **author's normative claim**, presented as such — not an empirical finding, an impossibility proof, or anything tested. It rests on the prior (also asserted) claim that the three axes are genuinely irreducible. The wiki asserts only that **the framework takes this position** — that value is multi-axial and single-scalar value models misrepresent it — not that the position is established. Notably, the named targets ("alignment," "optimisation") are AI-flavoured; the source does not cite a specific system, so the critique is read here as **general**, aimed at single-objective value modelling rather than any one implementation.

## Relationship to the framework's other "irreducibles"

This passage is one of three back-to-back structural admissions in *"From 44 to 200,"* and the source keeps them distinct — so the wiki should too:

- The [[three-irreducibles|three things the framework can't derive]] — **Moral Status** (Layer 7), **Consciousness** (Layer 12), **Being** (Layer 13) — are *gaps in derivation*: places where "the chain of derivation hits a wall." They concern what the framework **cannot explain**.
- [[the-permanent-tensions|The Permanent Tensions]] — Universal vs. Particular, Justice vs. Forgiveness, Tradition vs. Creativity, Authenticity vs. Virtue — are *unresolvable oppositions between things the framework already contains*. They concern values the framework **can name but refuses to rank**.
- The Three Independent Evaluative Axes are *distinct dimensions of judgement that cannot be merged into one*. They concern ways of judging the framework **can name but refuses to collapse**.

The three share a single instinct — refusal to flatten complexity into one answer — but they are not the same refusal. The irreducibles say *we can't explain everything*; the tensions say *we won't reconcile everything*; the axes say *we won't reduce all value to one number*. The axes are closest in spirit to the tensions (both are about refusing a false unification of values), and the source places them adjacently for exactly that reason; but the tensions are about *opposed* values that pull against each other, whereas the axes are about *different kinds* of value that simply do not share a scale.

## Why this sits in the wiki at all

Like the tensions, this is a philosophy-of-values passage with no direct engineering surface — yet it belongs because it is one concrete instance of the same anti-totalising instinct the first-party [[dark-factory]] synthesis adopts as an **epistemic guardrail**. Per `Dark Factory - Motive, Goal, Approach.md`, the Searles corpus's [[the-cult-test|Cult Test]] is used *"to prevent treating primitives as sacred truth,"* with *"the primitive framework [as] a tool, not a truth; the scientific method [as] the authority layer."* A refusal to collapse value into a single optimisation target is the value-modelling counterpart of that refusal to treat any one frame as the whole truth.

> ⚠ **Fail-legible note (the first-party tie is thin and interpretive).** The dark-factory docs do **not** mention the three evaluative axes, do not name "Practical / Moral / Aesthetic," and make no claim about single-metric value models; they cite only the broader Cult Test guardrail and the *"From 44 to 200"* post for the layers and irreducibles. The connection drawn here — the three axes as a *specific instance* of the framework-as-tool, don't-totalise guardrail — is **synthesis across two sources, not a claim either source makes explicitly.** Treat the three axes themselves as directly sourced; treat the tie to dark-factory doctrine as interpretive.
